# Minimum Information about Field Phenotyping

## Introduction

Scientific data are the foundation of modern research. Their value, however, depends not merely on the data themselves but on the contextual information — the metadata — that accompanies them and makes them interpretable, reproducible, and reusable. In recent decades, the life sciences have witnessed an explosion of data types: genomic sequences, transcriptional profiles, proteomic measurements, metabolomic datasets, and, more recently, high-throughput plant phenotyping observations. As the volume, diversity, and complexity of these datasets grew, so did awareness of a fundamental problem: without standardised, well-documented metadata, data that took enormous resources to produce could not be shared, compared, or reused effectively.
The concept of a Minimum Information (MI) standard emerged as a community-driven solution to this challenge. MI standards define the minimum set of metadata attributes necessary to fully describe an experiment, such that the data produced can be independently understood, evaluated, and reused by others. They are not prescriptive about methodology or analytical choice; rather, they establish a floor of descriptive completeness that facilitates scientific reproducibility and data interoperability.
This document traces the development of MI standards across biological disciplines, examines their purpose and evidence of measurable impact, and then narrows its focus to the Minimum Information About a Plant Phenotyping Experiment (MIAPPE) standard — the leading metadata framework for the plant phenotyping community.

## The Concept and Purpose of Minimum Information Standards

The rationale for minimum information standards is straightforward: to interpret and reproduce an experiment, one must understand not only the raw data but also the conditions under which those data were produced. This includes information about the biological material, the experimental design, the environmental conditions, the analytical techniques, the software and instruments used, and the people and institutions involved. Without this contextual metadata, data become difficult to interpret in isolation and effectively impossible to integrate with data from other studies.

Taylor et al. (2008) articulated this need at a community level with the founding of the Minimum Information for Biological and Biomedical Investigations (MIBBI) project. The MIBBI authors observed that, while individual research communities had begun developing their own MI checklists, these efforts had proliferated independently, creating redundancy, inconsistency in terminology, and barriers to cross-disciplinary data integration [1]. The MIBBI project provided a "one-stop shop" — a portal and collaborative framework — to catalogue existing MI checklists, foster their coordinated development, and identify shared informational concepts across disciplines.

A closely related and highly consequential development was the formulation of the FAIR Data Principles by Wilkinson et al. (2016) in Scientific Data [2]. FAIR — which stands for Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, and Reusable — established a concise and measurable set of guidelines for data publication. While FAIR principles are aspirational (describing qualities rather than mandating specific formats), they have provided the overarching governance framework within which MI standards operate. Rich, standardised metadata are the practical mechanism by which data become FAIR: a phenotyping dataset published with full MIAPPE-compliant metadata is, by design, more findable through semantic indexing, more interpretable by both humans and machines, and more readily reusable in downstream analyses.

##  Development of Minimum Information Standards Across Disciplines
### Genomics and Microarray Analysis: MIAME
The landmark contribution that galvanised the wider MI standards movement came from the microarray community. In 2001, Brazma et al. published the Minimum Information About a Microarray Experiment (MIAME) standard in Nature Genetics [3]. MIAME specified six categories of information required to interpret a microarray experiment unambiguously: the raw data (hybridisation intensities), final processed data, protocols, sample information, experimental factors, and experimental design. The authors' stated goal was to establish a standard for recording and reporting microarray-based gene expression data, which would in turn facilitate the development of public data repositories and analysis tools.
MIAME became one of the most influential MI standards in the history of biology. It was adopted as a mandatory submission requirement by major journals including Nature and its family of journals, and its implementation underpinned the construction of foundational public databases such as the NCBI Gene Expression Omnibus (GEO) and ArrayExpress at the European Bioinformatics Institute (EMBL-EBI). By 2008, the MIAME paper had accumulated thousands of citations, and compliance with MIAME had become a de facto quality standard for published microarray data [1].

### Genomics and Sequencing: MIGS and MIxS
Building on the success of MIAME, the genomics community developed analogous standards for sequence data. The Minimum Information about a Genome Sequence (MIGS) specification addressed the need for standardised reporting of genomic sequences submitted to public repositories. This was subsequently expanded into the Minimum Information about any (x) Sequence (MIxS) family of standards, which provides a modular framework accommodating diverse sequence types, from genomes to metagenomes to environmental samples. These standards were coordinated through the Genomic Standards Consortium (GSC) and referenced by MIBBI as exemplars of disciplinary MI checklist development [1].

### Proteomics: MIAPE
The proteomics community developed the Minimum Information About a Proteomics Experiment (MIAPE), coordinated through the Human Proteome Organisation Proteomics Standards Initiative (HUPO-PSI). MIAPE established standards for the description of mass spectrometry experiments, protein identification workflows, and quantification methods. Its development closely followed the MIAME model, with an explicit aim of facilitating data deposition in proteomics repositories such as PRIDE at EMBL-EBI. As noted in the MIBBI paper, MIAPE exemplified how domain-specific MI checklists could be constructed to reflect the unique informational requirements of a particular technology, while sharing common structural elements with standards in other fields [1].

### Metabolomics: CIMR
Metabolomics, as the youngest of the major omics disciplines, followed a similar trajectory. The Coordination of Standards in Metabolomics (COSMOS) initiative and related efforts developed the Chemical Information for Metabolomics Research (CIMR) standard. Metabolomics presents particular challenges for metadata standardisation because the diversity of metabolomic platforms (NMR, LC-MS, GC-MS) and the heterogeneity of the chemical entities measured require a rich and flexible descriptive vocabulary. The CIMR and related efforts were instrumental in establishing the MetaboLights repository at EMBL-EBI, and are referenced as part of the broader MIBBI family of standards [1].

### Systems Biology and Biochemical Models: MIRIAM
Beyond experimental data, MI standards also extended to computational models in systems biology. The Minimum Information Requested in the Annotation of Biochemical Models (MIRIAM) specified the metadata required to unambiguously identify and reproduce a mathematical model of a biological system. MIRIAM was motivated by the same reproducibility concerns that drove experimental MI standards: a published model that cannot be uniquely identified, retrieved, and executed is of limited scientific value. The MIRIAM standard underpins the BioModels database, which serves as a community repository for curated computational models [1].

### Clinical and Epidemiological Research: EQUATOR and CONSORT
The impulse to define minimum reporting requirements has not been confined to molecular biology. In clinical research, the EQUATOR (Enhancing the QUAlity and Transparency Of health Research) network has coordinated the development of reporting guidelines — most notably CONSORT for randomised trials, STROBE for observational studies, and PRISMA for systematic reviews. The MIBBI authors explicitly positioned their project in dialogue with EQUATOR, recognising that both initiatives addressed the same fundamental problem: the inadequate description of study design and methods in published research, with consequent harm to reproducibility and secondary use of data [1].
